Op-Ed

Topic: Flight Nurse Career Pathway and Requirements in New South Wales
# The Flight Nurse Pathway: A Career Worth Fighting For—If We Get the Support Right

Australia's flight nurses occupy a peculiar position in healthcare: they're among our most skilled emergency practitioners, yet they remain chronically undervalued and undersupported. The pathway to becoming a flight nurse in New South Wales is rigorous, demanding, and frankly, unnecessarily complicated. It's time we acknowledged both the excellence this profession requires and the systemic failures that make it harder than it should be.

Let's be clear about what we're asking of these professionals. To work as a flight nurse in NSW, you must first hold APHRA registration as a nurse and possess post-registration qualifications in critical care or emergency nursing.[5][6] You'll need current Advanced Life Support (ALS) and Paediatric Advanced Life Support (PALS) certifications, with some roles demanding Neonatal Advanced Resuscitation certification as well.[1] Many employers prefer—and some require—the Certified Flight Registered Nurse (CFRN) examination, which focuses on basic and advanced life support, pediatric care, advanced trauma, and emergency nursing.[2] This is not a casual credential. This is a professional who has invested years in education, maintained multiple certifications, and proven their ability to function in high-stakes, unpredictable environments.

And yet, here's where my frustration begins: the system that produces these highly qualified professionals often treats them as interchangeable with standard nurses. The entry requirements themselves reveal a troubling contradiction. While the Royal Flying Doctor Service (RFDS) explicitly requires a **minimum of three years full-time equivalent experience in a Primary Health Care setting**, including rural and remote nursing experience,[1] other aeromedical operators appear to have more flexible thresholds. This inconsistency suggests we haven't yet settled on what we actually value in this role. Do we want generalists who've worked in remote communities? Do we want specialists trained in emergency departments? The answer should be both, but our current framework doesn't clearly articulate why.

## The Certification Maze: Necessary Rigor or Bureaucratic Burden?

Here's an uncomfortable opinion: we're probably over-certifying in some areas and under-certifying in others. The requirement for multiple advanced life support certifications is defensible—these skills genuinely matter when you're treating a patient at 10,000 feet. But the fragmentation of these requirements across different employers creates unnecessary friction. A flight nurse working for CareFlight might need different certifications than one working for the RFDS, despite performing fundamentally similar work.[3] This isn't just inconvenient; it wastes professional development resources that could be directed toward genuine skill advancement.

The CFRN examination is valuable, but it shouldn't be gatekeeping. Some employers don't require it, which is sensible—a nurse with five years of emergency department experience might be more capable than a newly certified CFRN graduate. Yet the certification's existence creates an implicit hierarchy that pressures candidates to pursue it regardless. I'd argue for a more flexible approach: certifications should be recognized as valuable credentials without becoming de facto requirements that inflate the cost and timeline of entry.

## The Elephant in the Room: Compensation and Support

Here's what really troubles me about this profession: the RFDS offers tangible support that other sectors don't adequately match. Relocation allowances up to $7,000, remote area housing assistance with 50% reduction in taxable value for FBT, and remote area travel assistance—these aren't luxuries, they're acknowledgments that we're asking professionals to work in challenging conditions.[1] Yet these benefits remain concentrated in rural and remote operations. What about flight nurses working in urban aeromedical services? Why should geographic location determine whether you receive adequate support?

My opinion here is direct: we need industry-wide standardization of flight nurse compensation and benefits. The current patchwork approach means talented nurses might choose roles based on financial incentives rather than where they'd be most effective. That's not just unfair to individuals; it's inefficient healthcare policy.

## The Career Progression Question

One of the most glaring omissions in the available information is the lack of clarity around career progression. The research identified flight nurse roles, but where do they lead? Can a flight nurse transition into management, specialized trauma care, or aeromedical education? The RFDS mentions PHC-Midwifery Scholarships for internal staff,[1] which is encouraging, but this appears to be an exception rather than a norm. If we want to retain experienced flight nurses—and we desperately should—we need visible pathways to advancement that don't require leaving the profession.

## A Profession Worth Investing In

Despite these criticisms, I want to be emphatic: the flight nurse career pathway, as it currently exists, attracts and develops exceptional professionals. The combination of rigorous entry requirements, ongoing certification demands, and the inherent challenges of aeromedical work creates a self-selecting group of highly capable practitioners. These are people who've chosen not just nursing, but emergency nursing, but flight operations, but remote medicine. That's a remarkable level of commitment.

The real question isn't whether we should maintain high standards—we absolutely should. The question is whether we're willing to invest adequately in the professionals who meet them. We ask flight nurses to work in unpredictable environments, maintain multiple certifications, manage complex equipment, and make life-or-death decisions with minimal margin for error. They deserve compensation, support structures, and career pathways that reflect that reality. Until we provide them, we'll continue to see talented professionals either burn out or migrate to better-supported roles in other countries. That's not a failure of individuals; it's a failure of policy. And it's one we can fix, if we choose to.
